Introduction and About Me

There’s nothing more humbling than skateboarding. It’s a cruel sport: the concrete is hard, the falls hurt more each time, and even the littlest things — a pebble, speed wobbles, or a crack – can leave you bruised.

Skateboarding can also be one of the most exhilarating, mind-numbing, and satisfying things to do consistently. When you get comfortable on the board, you progress quickly. The basics build on themselves and, eventually, you piece together tricks. Then you piece together lines of tricks. Each one more difficult than the last. With that said, it all starts with the basics. Before you try tricks, it's essential to build up confidence on your board, practice stopping, turning, and riding down curbs.

Before we start, here's little about me: My name is Andrew – I love to skate big hills like in my home-city, San Francisco, to push my limits, and share the sport with as many people as possible. As such, I made this website dedicated to the basics of How to Skateboard.

Basics Review

  • PUSH: put your dominant foot on the front bolts of your board with your front toe pointed forward, lock your knee, and push with your opposite foot.
  • TURN: there are two beginner methods to turn.
    1. Classic Carve: standing with your feet on the board's bolts (above the wheels), shift your weight between the balls of your feet and your heels, leaning with the curve. BONUS! Carve down big hills to slow down!
    2. Kick-turn: put your back foot on the board's tail end, push down lightly, and swivel your front hip left and right, depending on the direction you want to go. Once your nose has adjusted, add pressure to your front foot so that board's nose hits the ground pointed in a new direction.
  • STOP: the hardest part of skateboarding. There are two easy ways to stop or slow down.
    1. Stomp: going down a hill, lock your front knee and swivel your hips so that your front toe faces toward. Then, with a sturdy front knee, stomp your pushing-foot into the group. This will help your slow down, but it takes a lot of control to stop this way.
    2. Tail Drag: push a lot of your weight onto your back foot, causing the nose to pop up and your tail to drag against the ground. Push into your back foot, lean, forward, and try to keep your shoulders facing forward as your grind to a hault.
  • BAIL: when a clean stop fails, there isn't any shame in bailing off your board – but do it wisely! If you jump off the tail/nose or kick the board behind you then you're in for a big wipeout. It's very important to evenly distribute your weight between front and back bolt and jump UP, not out. I would recomend practicing how to bail BEFORE skating down hills or going too far out of your comfort zone.

Get after it!

Learning to skate might mean lots of falls, blood, bruises, rolled ankles and getting back up when you each sh*t in front of your friends or worse, your crush. But by persevering through that, the skateboarder is blessed with an extraordinary outlet for their creativity, to relieve stress, engage a like-minded community, and to improve themselves in an arena that demands perserverance, grit, and discipline. Now get out there and get the board rolling!
